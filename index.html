Sartre, Freedom, Bad Faith, and Hope

Organized Notes & Reflections

Exploring how hope, freedom, anguish, and bad faith intertwine.

Guiding Question
Is hope anguish?
1. Starting Thoughts and Statements
  • Life is weird, absurd, and filled with the anguish of existence.
  • We are condemned to be free; freedom is unavoidable and carries responsibility.
    “I am condemned to be free.” (Sartre, 1956, p. 439)
    “... condemned, because he did not create himself, yet nonetheless free, because once cast into the world, he is responsible for everything he does.” (Sartre, 2007,p. 29)
  • Freedom generates both possibility and anxiety, because I must choose without guarantees(Sartre, 2007,p. 25).
  • Hope and freedom are mutually sustaining:
    • Hope gives rise to freedom, because one must believe in possibility to act.
    • Freedom gives rise to hope, because one must be able to choose and imagine otherwise.
2. Bad Faith and Inauthenticity
  • Bad faith: allowing an identity, role, system, or expectation to define you as a fixed thing.
    • Example: the waiter who becomes the role instead of a person who works as a waiter.
      “I can not be he. I can only play at being him. I affect him with nothingness.” (Sartre, 1956, p. 121)
      “Their condition is wholly one of ceremony.” (Sartre, 1956, p. 121)
      “I am a waiter in the mode of being what I am not.” (Sartre, 1956, p. 61)
  • Bad faith is:
    • denying your ongoing potentiality,
    • pretending your identity is completed, finished, and unchangeable,
    • holding onto specific attributes as if no decisions remain.(Lenzi, 2025, p.203)
  • Bad faith is comforting because:
    • it is a leap of faith that assumes no change will occur,
    • it relieves responsibility: “I am this”,
    • it avoids anguish by reducing uncertainty.
      “The only way to evade that disturbing thought is through some kind of bad faith.” (Sartre, 2007, p. 25)
3. Capitalism, Necessity, and the Collapse of Hope
  • Capitalism suggests:
    • that everything is necessary and inevitable,
    • that roles are fixed and futures are predetermined.
  • When hope collapses, freedom collapses with it:
    • hope is replaced by necessity,
    • potential is replaced by a false identity.
    • Things are not what they seem. We are full of potential. Bad faith acts as if potential is not real.
  • Sartre rejects fixed human nature and any claim that everything is already decided.
    “... there is no human nature since there is no God to conceive of it.” (Sartre, 2007, p.23)
    “... we will encounter no values or orders that can legitimize our conduct.” (Sartre, 2007, p.29)
4. Hope: Simultaneous Freedom and Anguish
  • Hope is both freedom and anguish.
  • Hope can:
    • lock you into bad faith if you cling to a single desired outcome,
    • liberate you if it remains dynamic, open, and continual.
  • Hope is risk because it projects into a future that does not yet exist.
    “The being of consciousness is to exist by itself, then to make itself be and thereby to pass beyond itself.” (Sartre, 1956, p. 69)
    “at man is, before all else, something-thatprojects itselfinto a future, and is conscious of doing so.” (Sartre, 2007,p. 23)
  • We are always able to undo hopes, revise them, or abandon them. This possibility is part of what makes hope painful.
    “It is not a screen that separates us from action but a condition of action itself.” (Sartre, 2007, p.27)
  • No one and nothing requires us to hope for a “good life.”(Lenzi, 2025, p.161)
5. Nothingness and Becoming
  • Sartre: “Being for itself” is not completed, always in process.
    “On all sides I escape being and yet I am.” (Sartre, 1956, p. 61)
  • Nothingness allows:
    • reinvention,
    • responsibility,
    • self destruction,
    • transformation.
    “Freedom is the human being putting his past out of play by secreting his own nothingness.” (Sartre, 1956, p. 28)
  • Hope functions within nothingness:
    • Hope imagines a future self that does not yet exist.
    • Hope acknowledges that we are unfinished.
      “He will not be anything until later, and then he will be what he makes of himself.” (Sartre, 2007, p.22)
6. Hope, Growth, and Self-Consciousness
  • Growth is only possible if hope persists, because hope projects a self that is not yet real.
    “I apprehend myself simultaneously as totally responsible for my being and yet as totally unjustifiable.” (Sartre, 1956, p. 83)
  • Example:
    • Hoping to be accepted as a speaker produces anguish while waiting,
    • but that hope makes learning, preparing, and growth possible,
    • even if the hope fails, it can give rise to new forms of hope.
  • We hope others see us positively, so we behave toward that projection.
    “... there is not a single one of our actions that does not create an image of man as we think he ought to be.” (Sartre, 2007, p.24)
  • When others view us poorly, shame can generate new hope to transform how we live and act.
7. Self-Destruction and Freedom
  • We are free even to engage in self destructive acts. (Sartre, 2007, p. 29)
  • Hope does not guarantee positive outcomes. It only frames how I move into possibility.
  • Hope requires action to realize its imagined futures, and I remain responsible for the actions and passions that follow.(Sartre, 2007,p. 29)
8. My Final Position
  • Sartre seems to accept anguish, avoid bad faith, and acknowledge complete freedom.
  • My thoughts:
    • Hope is the antidote to bad faith.
    • To avoid becoming a static thing, one must maintain and renew hope.
    • Hope keeps the self open, dynamic, and capable of growth.
      Man is nothing other than his own project. (Sartre, 2007, p.10)
      “Freedom is the human being secreting his own nothingness.” (Sartre, 1956, p. 28)

My thesis statement:

We are condemned to be free and, in that same way, condemned to hope. Hope is not merely optimism but the ongoing refusal to be finished, fixed, or complete.
9. Distilled Thoughts
  • We are condemned to be free.
  • We are condemned to be hopeful.
  • Hope is our ongoing negotiation with nothingness.
  • Hope keeps us from becoming a thing.
  • To hope is to accept anguish, possibility, and growth.
