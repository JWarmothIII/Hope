Sartre, Freedom, Bad Faith, and Hope

Organized Notes & Reflections

Exploring how freedom, anguish, bad faith, and hope interlock in Sartre’s existentialism.

Guiding Question
Is hope anguish?
1. Starting Thoughts and Statements
  • Life is weird, absurd, and filled with the anguish of existence.
  • We are condemned to be free.
  • Freedom generates both possibility and anxiety.
  • Hope and freedom are mutually sustaining:
    • Hope gives rise to freedom, because one must believe in possibility to act.
    • Freedom gives rise to hope, because one must be able to choose and imagine otherwise.
2. Bad Faith and Inauthenticity
  • Bad faith: allowing an identity, role, system, or expectation to define you as a fixed thing.
    • Example: the waiter who becomes the role instead of a person who works as a waiter.
  • Bad faith is:
    • denying your ongoing potentiality,
    • pretending your identity is completed, finished, and unchangeable,
    • holding onto specific attributes as if no decisions remain.
  • Bad faith is comforting because:
    • it is a leap of faith that assumes no change will occur,
    • it relieves responsibility: “I am this”,
    • it avoids anguish by reducing uncertainty.
3. Capitalism, Necessity, and the Collapse of Hope
  • Capitalism suggests:
    • that everything is necessary and inevitable,
    • that roles are fixed and futures are predetermined.
  • When hope collapses, freedom collapses with it:
    • hope is replaced by necessity,
    • potential is replaced by a false identity.
“Things aren’t what they seem. We are full of potential.” Bad faith acts as if potential isn’t real.
4. Hope: Simultaneous Freedom and Anguish
  • Hope is both freedom and anguish.
  • Hope can:
    • lock you into bad faith if you cling to a single desired outcome,
    • liberate you if it remains dynamic, open, and continual.
  • Hope is risk.
  • We are always able to undo hopes, revise them, or abandon them.
  • No one and nothing requires us to hope for a “good life.”
5. Nothingness and Becoming
  • Sartre: “Being-for-itself” is not completed, always in process.
  • Nothingness allows:
    • reinvention,
    • responsibility,
    • self-destruction,
    • transformation.
  • Hope functions within nothingness:
    • Hope imagines a future self that does not yet exist.
    • Hope acknowledges that we are unfinished.
6. Hope, Growth, and Self-Consciousness
  • Growth is only possible if hope persists.
  • Example:
    • hoping to be accepted as a speaker produces anguish while waiting,
    • but that hope makes learning, preparing, and growth possible,
    • even if the hope fails, it births new hope.
  • We hope others see us positively, so we behave toward that projection.
  • When others view us poorly, shame generates newly formed hope to transform.
7. Self-Destruction and Freedom
  • We are free even to engage in self-destructive acts.
  • Hope does not guarantee positive outcomes.
  • Hope requires action to realize its imagined futures.
8. My Final Position
  • Sartre seems to accept anguish, avoid bad faith, and acknowledge complete freedom.
  • My thoughts:
    • Hope is the antidote to bad faith.
    • To avoid becoming a static thing, one must maintain and renew hope.
    • Hope keeps the self open, dynamic, and capable of growth.

My thesis statement:

We are condemned to be free and, in that same way, condemned to hope. Hope is not merely optimism but the ongoing refusal to be finished, fixed, or complete.
9. Distilled Thoughts
  • We are condemned to be free.
  • We are condemned to be hopeful.
  • Hope is our ongoing negotiation with nothingness.
  • Hope keeps us from becoming a thing.
  • To hope is to accept anguish, possibility, and growth.
